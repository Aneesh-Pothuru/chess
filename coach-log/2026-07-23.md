## Annotated game: pots1125 (676→707) vs unknowngirl181910 (722) — London, +6, mated move 24

The most expensive fifteen minutes of the day. You won a knight for free, were up six points of
material with six minutes on the clock, and got mated in the middle of the board.
[Game](https://www.chess.com/game/live/171942257842)

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 Nc6 4.Nf3 Bg4 5.Bd3 Bxf3 6.Qxf3 e6 7.c3 Bd6 8.Nd2 Bxf4 9.Qxf4 O-O** —
Textbook London against an early ...Nc6/...Bg4. Both minor-piece trades are fine for you.
Notice the scoreboard on move 9: Black has castled. You haven't. Nothing stands between your
king and g1 — O-O is legal right now and stays legal for the next nine moves.

**10.Qh4?!** — The move that decided the game, fourteen moves before the mate. The queen goes
hunting and the plan becomes g4-g5 — an attack launched with your own king still on e1. 10.O-O
first costs nothing: same attack available next move, king safe forever. Attack is a luxury;
castling is rent, and rent gets paid first.

**10...b6 11.g4 e5 12.g5 Ne4 13.Nxe4 dxe4 14.Bxe4 g6 15.Bxc6!** — Credit: this is alert chess.
Black's 10...b6 quietly took the b7-pawn's protection off the c6-knight, and when the position
opened, you took it — a free piece, and the bishop hits a8 on the way. **+4 and strategically
winning.**

**15...Rc8 16.f4 exd4 17.cxd4 Qd6 18.Rc1?!** — Still winning easily, but this is the last
moment O-O was possible, and instead the a1-rook stepped to c1. Ask the won-game question: "What
is her *only* idea here?" It's checks. Which square does Qd6 want? b4.

**18...Qb4+ 19.Kd1** — There's the tax. No piece can block (c3 and d2 are empty and unreachable),
so the king walks, the rooks disconnect, and every Black check from now on is a fork threat.

**19...Qxb2 20.Be4 Rfe8 21.Qh3?? Rxe4!** — The queen stepped off the fourth rank — the rank it
was defending the e4-bishop along. One hung piece per game is the tithe right now; this was
today's. Still +3 after **22.Qxc8+ Kg7 23.Re1 Qa3** — in fact after picking up the c8-rook
you're **+6**. Picture the position: your king on d1 with your own rooks on c1 and e1 pressed
against it like coffin walls. Black's queen on a3, rook on e4. One rank of air.

**24.Qxc7??** — Six points up, and the move played is a pawn grab on the far side of the board.
The question that was never asked: **"Where does her queen check me from?"** There is exactly
one answer — d3 — and it isn't a check, it's mate: c2, d2, e2 all covered by the queen, c1 and
e1 occupied by your own rooks. 24.Rc3! asks her queen to leave *and* covers d3 with the rook;
24.Rc2 or 24.Kc2 also hold easily. Any of them and the +6 converts itself.

**24...Qd3#** — Mated in the exact center of the board, up a rook, with 5:49 on the clock. The
uncastled king from move 10 and the unasked question from move 24 are the same mistake wearing
two masks: safety work postponed.

**The lesson to carry:** when you are winning, your move-selection question changes. It is no
longer "what does this move do for me?" — it is **"what is their next check or capture?"** Answer
that first, every move, and games like this one end 1-0 by move 30.
