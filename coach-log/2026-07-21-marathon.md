## Annotated game: pots1125 (White) vs hemendik-at — a winning London lost on the clock

You had this game won. You lost it on time with +2 still on the board. This is the single most instructive game of the day because nothing on the board beat you — the clock did.

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 e6 3.Nf3 c5 4.e3 Bd6** — Textbook London so far. Black offers to trade off your good bishop.

**5.c3 Bxf4 6.exf4** — Fine. Your pawn structure is a touch clumsy (doubled f-pawns) but the half-open e-file and the f4–e5 space are real compensation. Nothing wrong here.

**6…cxd4 7.cxd4 Nf6 8.Bd3 Qc7 9.Nc3 Qxf4?** — Black gets greedy and grabs the f4 pawn with the queen. You're down a pawn now (−1), but look at where his queen is: stuck on f4, far from home, begging to be chased. **This is the moment to be happy, not worried.** A misplaced enemy queen is worth more than a pawn.

**10.g3 Qc7 11.Nb5 Qd7 12.Rc1 O-O 13.Nc7** — There it is. The knight jumps to c7 and forks the a8-rook. This is you winning the material back and then some. Over the next dozen moves this knight and your active pieces harvest material until you're **+6 — a full piece up — by move 26 (Nxe6)**. On the board, this game is over. You've won it.

**The turning point isn't a move. It's your clock.** At move 26 you had **3:09** left. That is plenty to convert a piece up. Here is the whole lesson of the day:

**28.Bxg6+!? Kxg6 29.h5+** — You went looking for a forcing knockout, sacrificing the bishop to strip the king. It's not a *bad* move — you stayed winning (+4). But it cost you **two full minutes** of clock (3:07 → 0:59) to calculate, and it traded your safe, boring +6 for a sharp position you now had to find precise moves in **with under a minute left**. When you are up a piece, you do not need to sacrifice anything. You need to **trade the queens and rooks off** and push the position toward a K+pawns ending your opponent cannot save.

**30.g5 Rxe6 31.Rxe6 Kxe6 32.gxh6** — Still winning, but now you're under 0:35 and moving on fumes.

**33.Qa1 34.Qb1 35.Qc2 36.Qc5+ 37.Qb5+ — flag falls on move 37, position +2.** The queen shuffles at the end aren't losing moves; they're a player who has run out of clock making *any* legal move to survive one more second. The game was decided twenty moves earlier, not by a blunder, but by how you *spent* the 3 minutes you had while winning.

**The one lesson to carry into tomorrow:** When you win material — even just a clean pawn where the enemy queen is stranded — flip a switch in your head. Stop hunting for the prettiest finish. **Offer every trade, take every simplification, and move fast.** A piece up in a queenless endgame plays itself; a piece up with queens on and 30 seconds left does not. You didn't get outplayed today. You got out-clocked in games you had already won.
