## Annotated game: keroRoma (709) — pots1125 (697), Caro-Kann 2.Nc3

**1.e4 c6 2.Nc3 d5 3.exd5 cxd5 4.Nf3 Nc6 5.Bb5** — Repertoire ground. White's Bb5 pins
nothing yet, but it stares at the c6 knight, and that stare is the whole game.

**5...Bd7 6.O-O Nf6 7.d3** — Note the move order: White is castled by move 6; Black's
king is still home. Nothing is wrong yet — 7...e6 and 8...Be7 and 9...O-O and Black is
simply fine, a tempo up on a quiet position.

**7...e5?** — The move looks ambitious: grab the center. But run the count. The e5 pawn
is defended once (the c6 knight) — and White's bishop on b5 is *attacking that defender*.
**8.Bxc6 Bxc6 9.Nxe5** — defender removed, pawn gone. This is the "removed guard" motif
from your puzzle sets, played against you in the opening. One pawn isn't fatal, but every
problem for the next fifteen moves grows from this seed.

**9...Bd6 10.Nxc6 bxc6 11.Qe2+ Qe7 12.Qxe7+ Bxe7 13.Re1** — White trades everything
toward the ending where the extra pawn matters, and parks a rook on the e-file. Look at
the board: the only Black piece between that rook and the king on e8 is the bishop on e7.
That's the position screaming one word: **castle**.

**13...d4 14.Ne4 Nxe4 15.Rxe4 Rd8?** — Here is the decision that loses the game, twelve
moves before the mate. Castling was legal, free, and urgent. Instead the *other* rook
came to d8 — activity over safety. The d4 pawn it defends is worth less than the king on
e8. From now on every White move gains force because the king is a target on the e-file.

**16.Bg5 f6 17.Bf4 Rd7 18.Rae1 g5? 19.Bd6!** — The pawn lunges (...f6, ...g5) chased the
bishop into a better square. On d6 it hits e7 — and the e7 bishop is **pinned** by the
e4 rook against the king. Pinned pieces don't defend; the e7 bishop is already lost in
spirit.

**19...f5** — Counterattacking the rook, but White simply takes first.

**20.Rxe7+ Kf8?? 21.Rxd7+** — After 20...Rxe7 21.Bxe7 Kxe7 the damage was one pawn — a
worse-but-playable ending. Instead 20...Kf8 stepped into the d6-bishop's diagonal:
21.Rxd7 is *discovered check* (bishop d6–f8), winning the d7 rook with tempo.

**21...Kg8 22.Re8#** — Rook slides to the eighth, king sealed in by its own pawns on f7
— wait, f7 is empty; it's g7-less too. The king on g8 has g7 vacated by ...g5 and f-pawn
gone — but e8 covers the rank and the d6 bishop covers f8. No escape. A back-rank family
mate against a king that never castled.

**The lesson to carry:** two, today. First: **before ...e5 in the Caro, count the
defenders after the trade on c6** — Bb5 plus Nf3 means the break needs preparation
(...e6, ...Bd6/...Be7, ...O-O first). Second, and bigger: **when queens or rooks share a
file with your king, castling is not a developing move — it's the most forcing move on
the board.** Move 13, 14, and 15 were all castling moves that weren't played.
